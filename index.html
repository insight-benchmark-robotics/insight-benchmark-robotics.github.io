We formalize the task of "In-situ Guide Grounding": a robot's ability to interpret symbols and text physically attached to objects.

Abstract

Humans intuitively use on-object guides (e.g., "PULL", "Squeeze and Turn") to interact with environments. Our work introduces a diagnostic benchmark evaluating how VLA models ground these cues. Through 14k+ Expert Trajectories across five taxonomy categories, we show that while models succeed in simple selection, grounding directional and procedural guidance remains a major perception bottleneck.

A New Taxonomy of In-Situ Guides

01. Contact Affordance

Identifying which component is intended for interaction.

02. In-Part Specification

Detailing precise contact points (e.g., squeeze arrows).

03. Action Directional

Constraints on motion like CW/CCW rotation or push/pull.

04. Procedural Guidance

Multi-step operational sequences (e.g., "Rotate then Pull").

05. Target Disambiguation

Resolving goals among multiple candidate parts.

Real-World Validation

We validated our findings using a 7-DoF robotic arm. Simulation weights were fine-tuned via LoRA on a small real-world dataset (15 trajectories/task). The performance gap between visual-only (G-VLA) and language-instructed (LI-VLA) settings persists, confirming the challenge of interpreting embedded guides in the wild.

Experimental Insights

  • Perception Bottleneck: Models struggle to ground directional and procedural information from raw visual input alone.
  • Language as a Proxy: Performance jumps significantly when guides are provided as text (LI-VLA), showing the action policy itself is robust.
  • Visual Distraction: For smaller backbones, on-object guides can act as visual distractors, leading to a performance drop.
